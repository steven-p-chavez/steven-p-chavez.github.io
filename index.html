Steven Paul Chavez

I have a degree in Physics and I'm currently a Ph.D candidate at DUKE University in the department of Civil and Environmental Engineering. I'm doing research on cloud modeling revisiting the most fundamental physic-chemistry foundations of aerosols interactions with water and using a moist atmospheric model of very high resolution (50m). The moist model is a 2-dimensional large eddy simulation (includes k-theory of turbulence) and solves numerically the momemtun and thermodynamic equations in flux form (is conservative),  diffusion associated with turbulence and  the wind circulation mixes the atmosphere avoiding the need of parametrization of entrainment. To track the condensation of water over some aerosol I included bin microphysics,  which requieres an initial discretized (bin) distribution of aerosol properties obtained from measurements at the ground, depending on the atmospheric condition some aerosols get wet and condense enought water to become cloud droplets. The collition of cloud droplets of different sizes is modeled by a stochastic equation. The finals results are realistic clouds. The main objective of my reseach is to investigate the impact of atmospheric conditions and aerosol properties in the evolution of cloud droplet distribution. Knowing the distribution of cloud droplets is fundamental to determinate the cloud optical properties. These  optical properties impact the  cloud albedo effect  , which is   one of the largest sources of uncertainty in climate change estimations of radiative forcing.

Cloud modeling is a multi-scale challenge

Aerosol radius except for giant aerosol are mainly in the orden of 10-9 to 10-7 m. Rain droplets are in the order of 10-3 m. And cloud droplets are in the middle , i.e.,   10-6 to 10-4 m .     Building a model that includes    all those processes is a monumental project. A two step approach is commonly used to modelate precipitating clouds,     first assume cloud droplets distributions  and  then focusing on the growing of precipitating particles. However, in the case of warm clouds and rain   its possible   to solve for both clouds and precipitation using bin microphysics and a generalization of the collision processes without breakout.            

  • Aerosol Chemistry
    Aerosols properties such as: Hygroscopicity, radius, number concentration, etc. Are the input of the model.
  • Atmospheric Conditions
    A vertical profile of pressure, potential temperature, especific humidity, zonal   wind velocity  is used and its assumed homogeneity in the horizontal dimension.
  • Cloud properties
    Bin microphysics will produce a distribution of drop sizes and number concentrations. From there cloud properties like liquid water conten, reflectivity and cloud optical depth are calculated.
